Single variable function¶

$y = f(x)$

$y = f(x_0)+ \left(\dfrac{d f}{d x}\right)_{x=x_0} (x-x_0) + \cdots$

Multivariable function¶

$ \boldsymbol{x}= \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2\\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix}$ and $\boldsymbol{y}= \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2\\ \vdots \\ y_m \end{bmatrix} $
\begin{equation} \boldsymbol{y}=\boldsymbol{f}\left( \boldsymbol{x} \right) \end{equation}     \begin{equation}    \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2\\ \vdots \\ y_m \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} f_1\left( x_1,  x_2, \cdots x_n \right)\\ f_2 \left( x_1,  x_2, \cdots x_n \right)\\ \vdots \\ f_m \left( x_1,  x_2, \cdots x_n \right) \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}

Jacobian -- Derivative of Multivariable function¶

  \begin{equation} \boldsymbol{y} = \boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{x}_0)+ \left(\dfrac{\boldsymbol{\partial f}}{\boldsymbol{\partial x}}\right)_{\boldsymbol{x}=\boldsymbol{x}_0} (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{x}_0) + \cdots \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \dfrac{\boldsymbol{\partial f}}{\boldsymbol{\partial x}} = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n} \\ \dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_1} & \dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1} & \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}

Jacobian in total derivatives¶

Let $\boldsymbol{\delta x} = (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{x}_0)$ and $\boldsymbol{\delta y} = (\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{x}_0))$

\begin{equation} \boldsymbol{\delta y} = \left(\dfrac{\boldsymbol{\partial f}}{\boldsymbol{\partial x}}\right)_{\boldsymbol{x}=\boldsymbol{x}_0} \boldsymbol{\delta x} + \cdots \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \boldsymbol{\dot{y}} = \left(\dfrac{\boldsymbol{\partial f}}{\boldsymbol{\partial x}}\right) \boldsymbol{\dot{x}} \end{equation}

Let $\boldsymbol{a}_1$, $\boldsymbol{a}_2$, ... $\boldsymbol{a}_n$ be the columns of the Jacobian matrix, i.e.,

$ \boldsymbol{a}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}\\ \dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_1}\\ \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1} \end{bmatrix} $,

$\boldsymbol{a}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_2}\\ \dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_2}\\ \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_2} \end{bmatrix}$, ... $\boldsymbol{a}_n = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}\\ \dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_n}\\ \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix} $

\begin{equation} \boldsymbol{\dot{y}} = \boldsymbol{a}_{1} \dot{x}_1 + \boldsymbol{a}_{2} \dot{x}_2 + \cdots \boldsymbol{a}_{n} \dot{x}_n \end{equation}

Observation: If we already have the expression for total (time) derivative of the multivariable function, then the columns of the Jacobian Matrix are the coefficients of time derivatives of the independent variables </font>¶

# transformation of i frame with respect to (i-1)' frame'
def joint_transform(d_i, theta_i):
    Joint_T = sy.Matrix([[sy.cos(theta_i),  -sy.sin(theta_i), 0, 0], 
                        [sy.sin(theta_i),  sy.cos(theta_i), 0, 0], 
                        [0, 0, 1, d_i],
                        [0,0,0,1] ])
    return Joint_T

 def cross_product(a,b):
        c=sy.Matrix([
            [a[1,0]*b[2,0]-a[2,0]*b[1,0]],
            [a[2,0]*b[0,0]-a[0,0]*b[2,0]],
            [a[0,0]*b[1,0]-a[1,0]*b[0,0]]
            ])
        return c

A brief review of rank of a matrix and its significance¶

Consider the matrix equation \begin{equation} \boldsymbol{b} = \boldsymbol{M} \boldsymbol{a} \end{equation}

  • Further suppose that $\boldsymbol{b}$ represents velocity of a point with the three elements of the matrix reprsenting $x$, $y$ and $z$ components.

  • Any value of $\boldsymbol{b}$ can be represented as a point in a cartesian coordinate system with $x$, $y$, $z$ axes representing $x$, $y$ and $z$ components of velocity

  • We can also think of $\boldsymbol{b}$ as a vector which starts from the origin and ends at the point

  • Let $\boldsymbol{M} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$, and $\boldsymbol{a} = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 \end{bmatrix} $

  • Assume that $\alpha_1$ can take any real value.

  • If we consider the set of all possible values for $\boldsymbol{b}$ and plot them in a cartesian velocity graph, the plot would be a one dimensional straight line.

  • Let $\boldsymbol{M} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$, and $\boldsymbol{a} = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 \end{bmatrix} $

  • Assume that $\alpha_1$ and $\alpha_2$ can take any real value.

  • If we consider the set of all possible values for $\boldsymbol{b}$ and plot them in a cartesion velocity graph, what would be dimension of the plot? Is it one dimensional or two dimensional?

  • To answer the question, let us plot the vectors corrsponding to the columns of the matrix

In [29]:
from IPython.display import IFrame

IFrame(src='./two_vectors.html', width=700, height=600)
Out[29]:
  • From the plot we see that the two vectors are distinct.
  • The set of all possibilities for $\boldsymbol{b}$ would be a plane containing these two vectors.
  • The plane is two dimensional
  • Hence the set of possibilities is two dimesnional.
  • $\boldsymbol{b}$ can have any direction along the plane.
  • The motion abilities or manouverability in second case is higher compared to first case.
  • In first case velocity or small motion could be only along a particular direction.
  • In the second case velocity or small motion could be along any direction in the plane.
  • The dimension of the possibilities of $\boldsymbol{b}$ indicates the degree of maneuverability for small motion.
  • In a general system $\boldsymbol{b} = \boldsymbol{M} \boldsymbol{a}$, what linear algebraic quantity indicates the dimension of possibilities of $\boldsymbol{b}$.
  • Is it number of columns of $\boldsymbol{M}$?
  • No, it is the rank of $\boldsymbol{M}$ that indicates the dimension of possibilities of $\boldsymbol{b}$.
  • How do you find rank of a matrix?
  • From first principle, you could find rank from row-reduced echelon form which you might have studied in a maths course.
  • Here we will see how to find the rank using sympy python library.
  • We find what is called as column space of the matrix with sympy.
  • The number of columns in columnspace is the rank. An illustrative example is given next.

The number of column space has turned out to be two. Hence the rank is two.

Rank of Jacobian Matrix¶

  • Similar to the example of previous slides, in equation $\begin{bmatrix} ^{0}\boldsymbol{v}_{4} \\ ^{0} \boldsymbol{\omega}_{4} \end{bmatrix}={}^{0}\boldsymbol{J}_{4} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{\theta}_{1} \\ \dot{\theta}_{2}\\ \dot{\theta}_{3} \\ \dot{\theta}_{4} \end{bmatrix}$, the dimension of possibilities for $\begin{bmatrix} ^{0}\boldsymbol{v}_{4} \\ ^{0} \boldsymbol{\omega}_{4} \end{bmatrix}$ is given by the rank of ${}^{0}\boldsymbol{J}_{4}$.

  • Since $\begin{bmatrix} ^{0}\boldsymbol{v}_{4} \\ ^{0} \boldsymbol{\omega}_{4} \end{bmatrix}$ indicates linear and angular velocity of last frame, the rank ${}^{0}\boldsymbol{J}_{4}$ would also indicate the degree of maneuverability of last frame in both position and orientation.

If we want the robot to have high maneuverability, we should operate the robot in such configurations where the rank of Jacobian Matrix is high.¶

Let us

  • The previous video showed that Jacobian of configuration lying on the boundary of workspace has smaller rank (3 in this case) compared to the rank of Jacobian whose configuration is lying inside the workspace.

  • This observation is not a coincidence. The loss of rank at the boundary is due to loss of ability of the end-link to move away from the boundary.

  • At the boundary, the robot would typically be at fully stretched condition or fully shrunk condition. Without further stretching (which might require breaking of link), motion ability along a particular direction is not possible. This is the reason for loss of manoeuvrability and hence loss of rank.

  • Many of the martial art poses have bent postures (not fully stretched) to give maximum manoeuvrability. For example see the image below:

  • The way we are considering Jacobians, it has six rows.
  • The robot that we considerd has four columns since there are four joint variables
  • However, robots like PUMA robot has six joints and Jacobian would be 6x6 square matrix.
    • The Jacobian would typically be full rank and hence invertible matrix
    • Under certain configuration, the jacobian could become rank deficient and hence loose invertibility. Non-invertible square matricies are called as singular matricies. The configuration that has rank deficient jacobian is called as singular configuration. Alternate way of saying is that the configuration has singularity.
    • If the loss of rank is due to being at the boundary of workspace, then it is called as Workspace-boundary singularity.
    • If the loss of rank happens interior to the work space, then it is called as Workspace-interior singularity. This singularity is typically caused by a lining-up of two or more joint axes.
